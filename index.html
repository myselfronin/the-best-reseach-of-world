1. Introduction

(Rabinson)

Software integration from kitchens to cars, for the benefit of human civilization, is advancing at a rapid rate. One can find software in virtually everything used in daily life. The field has stretched itself eminently by changing the way we govern, gain education, manage health, organize businesses, and so on. This means a huge amount of software has and is being developed every day. Along with the growth, the complexity of software products has also increased remarkably. The development process has become vulnerable to missing deadlines, low quality, cost overruns, and so on. Such problems or challenges must be overcome so as to prosper the whole field of software engineering toward a better future.

Along with the intricacy of software, the optimization of software phenomena or even finding effective ones has become a challenging task. Nevertheless, it is crucial for companies or organizations to continuously improve their software process in order to deliver high-quality software within a specified timeframe, budget, and with available resources. A systematic and disciplined approach to improving such phenomena are needed. Additionally, thousands of research for the sake of improvement are proposed but most of them lack or have low quality for validation. Such proposals can be evaluated using empirical methods which are best suited as the practitioner's perspective is the solid acceptance of proposed improvements.

This paper highlights the major empirical methods that can be used to perform an evaluation in software engineering. It also features the advantages and disadvantages of those methods. Also, we have discussed some of the importance of evaluation in software engineering in the later section before presenting a demonstration of our findings on performing surveys and interviews as strategies for those methods.

2. Empirical Software Engineering Evaluation

(Rabinson)

One of the pioneers of the SE research community, Robert Glass[4] has summarized four methods for research in the field of software engineering i.e. Scientific Method, Engineering Method, Empirical Method, and Analytical Method. In Scientific Method, the the world is observed and a model is built on which the research object is evaluated; Engineering Method is about proposing changes to current solutions and then evaluating them; The analytical method is used when a formal theory is proposed and is compared with empirical observation; in Empirical Method a model is evaluated empirically.

Although other methods are also necessary for software engineering, the focus of this paper is on Empirical Methods. Software engineering is driven by the professionals involved in software development. In order to evaluate the software phenomena, there needs a study on how software practitioners solve software problems ie discover their instincts or experience on the topic under discussion. Such phenomena can be processes, tools, techniques, policies, practices, and so on, used for developing software. Evaluating such phenomena using empirical strategies can be termed Empirical Evaluation in Software Engineering.

2.1 Empirical Methods

(Rabinson)

Qualitative and Quantitative are two main types of research paradigms in empirical studies[4]. Qualitative is concerned with understanding the point of view and discovering the notices of the subject. The subject would mean the person participating in the empirical studies for evaluating an object. These are informed by the qualitative data. On the other hand, Quantitative is concerned with comparing two or more groups to identify cause-effect relationships. As this is informed by quantitative data, one can perform comparison and statistical analysis on them. Both quantitative and qualitative research investigations can be implemented on the same topic. However, instead of being opposite poles, these method represents the different end of a continuum[1].

Traditionally, these two research are differentiated by closed-ended questions and responses, giving us a numerical result (quantitative) or open-ended questions and responses resulting in answers in words (qualitative) [3]. For example, when evaluating the code review process, one can ask about its effectiveness which gives answers on a range scale (i.e numbers). Whereas, others can inquire about ways to make the code review process more effective. The formal question is a part of quantitative research and the latter is for qualitative research. But a complete way to see the difference between these two is to look at the basic assumptions, the types of research strategies used, and the method for conducting those strategies [3]. A study can be more quantitative and less qualitative in some and for others, it is vice-versa.

2.2 Importance of Evaluation in Research Process

(Rabinson)

In order to assess the validity of techniques or methods, evaluation of such a proposal is critical. If proper evidence or proof of new or improvised conventions is available then the software practitioner would get more confidence in using them. It enables us to set standards in working with a common problem and drive the software space more scientifically. Rather than a random instinct or guess for developing solutions, we should use scientifically validated methodologies which leads to a systematic improvement of the whole process. The need for the usage of empirically evaluated engineering is more essential for us than ever because of the fast-growing and challenging software industry. Our decision for utilizing certain tools or processes is driven by the evaluation present when such tools or processes are proposed. This not only contributes to methodical improvement but the overall enhancement of software engineering.

Unfortunately, the condition of evaluation in research within computer science or software engineering is not satisfactory. Additionally, the software practitioners themselves are not looking for the validity of the new ways proposed in this research area. According to from critics: "SE research suffers from irrelevance. Research outputs have little relevance to software practice."[6]. Even after a half-century of the existence in this industry, scientific development is still lacking. We would like to highlight some results from a research published by Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) in the year 2009[8]. In this research, they have categorized some random papers and papers specific to software engineering, based on the categories introduced by similar research in the year 1995[7]. They have then compared their own findings and results from the previous paper. It can be noted that there had been increasing in the reuse of work done by other researchers. In 1995 research it found that 15% of papers inside design and model did not have an evaluation section. And 67% of sampled papers had only one-fifth space dedicated. Whereas, research in 2009 found one-third of papers introducing new entities had not presented an evaluation, and 28% of them (in empirical and design & model) did not have an evaluation section.

3 Quantitative Method

(Frenkli)

Quantitative research approach uses objective theories and tests them by analyzing different variables and the relationship between them. These kinds of variables are measured using instruments so numerical data can be collected and used to produce statistical results. The researcher in this approach builds the research against the bias, eliminates or controls new nuances of explanations and findings, and builds a research that can be replicated and generalized [3](41).

In a quantitative approach, the researcher avoids the variation during the introduction of the topic since the objective is to what factors and variables influence an outcome and not to generate new ideas or solutions based on the participants perspective. For example, quantitative research can be conducted to understand if the financial issues contribute to a divorce. In this case, the variables and factors help the research to understand and explain this particular problem. The quantitative research introduction should not be written and affected by the personal view of the researcher in order to support objectivity [3](162).

3.1 Quantitative Methodologies

(Frenkli)

The researcher, despite the fact that has to choose between the quantitative, qualitative and mixed research approach, also decides on what type of design to choose within these approaches. Research methodologies are different kinds of inquiry that provide instructions and direction for procedures to be followed in a study. These types of design have grown dramatically over the years since the advances made in technology and tools to collect and analyze complex data [3](49).

3.1.1 Field experiments

(Frenkli)

Field experiments are conducted in actual environments. To test the effect, one or more factors must be isolated and changed. Although there are many more variables to take into account, it enables the researcher to witness more natural behavior. This method is also frequently used in applied sciences like bioengineering and medicine as well as sociology. When contrasted to lab tests, field experiments provide a number of advantages. It provides a setting that is more similar to nature than a lab. It is appropriate to examine large groups of people since they tend to be more representative. On the other side, controlling variables is more complicated, making it very difficult to reproduce the same conditions. Furthermore, because it is challenging to manage the environment, unforeseen events may occur and have a negative impact on patients and participants[5](380).

3.1.2 Surveys

(Frenkli)

A survey is a research method that enables the direct gathering of data from a participant in the study using a series of questions arranged in a certain order. It is one of the most popular quantitative techniques because it enables the gathering of data on a particular topic by asking questions that capture the attitudes, perceptions, and actions of a group of people. Surveys have a number of advantages. The high population representativeness and the method's inexpensive cost in comparison to other options are two of its most significant advantages. On the other hand, the survey's design and the respondents' accuracy of response depend greatly on the validity of the data [5](381).

3.1.3 Correlation study

(Frenkli)

In essence, correlational research is an experimental technique used to examine if there is a correlation between two or more variables. There is merely an examination of how closely the variables are related; there is no manipulation of the variables. A correlational analysis emphasizes two characteristics: the relationship's strength and direction. We can quickly quantify observational data by looking at the correlation coefficient. At the same time, we can gather a variety of data from numerous domains at once and investigate the relationships between those variables. The link between two variables could perhaps be explained by a third variable, hence correlation does not imply causality [5](381).

4. Qualitative Method

(Frenkli)

Qualitative research approach is used for understanding the perspective of individuals and their experience on a social problem and exploring a topic for different nuances. The process of conducting this kind of research approach involves data collected from participants in different life situations and experience which is represented by the interpretation of the researcher. The focus is on the individual meaning rather than numbers and statistics [3](41).

In a qualitative approach, the problem that the researcher describes can be understood by exploring a topic and comprehending new variations given by the participants. Qualitative research problem has some characteristics: the topic has a lack of previous research and theory which may be incorrect, inaccurate or biased; the topic needs to be developed further; a quantitative approach is not suitable for this kind of problem. In a qualitative approach, the researcher should rely on the participant's perspective and may begin the introduction of the problem with a personal opinion or experience from the researcher in order to focus on the subjective point of view [3](162).

4.1 Qualitative Methodologies

(Frenkli)

In qualitative approaches, the design types became more visible and clear during the 1990s. The origins for these approaches come from sociology, humanities, and evaluation. There are many methodologies and design types for qualitative research but we will focus on observations, ethnography and structured interviews and show their strengths and weaknesses [3](50).

4.1.1 Observation

(Frenkli)

Researchers examine a specific phenomena in its natural setting as part of a systematic method for gathering information called observation. This approach is particularly recommended when a subject is largely unknown and it is crucial to fully comprehend a phenomenon while preserving the surrounding circumstances. Observation is a useful method for gathering information concurrently with the occurrence of the event without interfering with it. It is a discrete, highly adaptable strategy that is focused on knowledge discovery. In some circumstances, such as the study of human and animal behavior, trustworthy data collection is the only option. On the other hand, it is a very time-consuming strategy that needs advance planning and the researcher's availability to travel to the location where the event occurs. Additionally, since the researcher is the only one who interprets the data, the method is extremely sensitive to the independence of the analysis [5](376).

4.1.2 Ethnography

(Frenkli)

In ethnography, people are interviewed after being observed in a circumstance. In an ethnographic study, the researcher makes an effort to understand the scenario being observed from the participants' points of view. There are two fundamental elements of ethnography: (i) the observation occurs in a natural context, and (ii) researchers must comprehend how an event is experienced and processed by members of a speech community. The methodologies of observation and ethnography are extremely similar. However, when doing an ethnographic study, the researcher must adopt a more comprehensive perspective and pay close attention to every detail [5](376).

The main benefit of ethnography is that it allows the researcher to gain a thorough understanding of the issue under study. However, ethnography involves a significant time commitment from the researcher, and because the study's findings can be so varied, it can be challenging to draw accurate and focused conclusions.

4.1.3 Structured Interviews

(Frenkli)

Structured interviews are a type of assessment tool used to collect and compare interviewee replies. The interviewee is questioned about prior encounters and/or suggested fictitious settings. All interviewees go through the same procedure. High response rates are normal for structured interviews, and the interviewer is present to explain the question to prevent respondents from misinterpreting it. A systematic interview might take time to prepare, though. Additionally, even if during the interview new lines of study sound fascinating to pursue, they do not have the same flexibility in the choice of the responses that are predefined and cannot be changed by the researcher [5](377).

5 Demo

(Rabinson)

In this research, we conducted a quantitative and qualitative method about the code review process. Code review is an important process for software evaluation and improvement nowadays. This process is relatively new and some more insight is needed on what developers think about this process by conducting interviews for the qualitative part. In order to support this data, we conducted surveys and analyzed the results to provide some statistical information.

The hypothesis of this evaluation is that the code review process is extremely important so we need to prove this by quantitative data and also get some detailed responses and experiences from professionals related to the software industry. We also want to find what tools and methods are more common in the code review process. The qualitative session will also provide us with different nuances of what code review process means from the participants’ perspective.

5.1 Audience

(Rabinson)

We focused our research on professionals in the software industry with different levels of experience, from junior to senior. This allowed us to see how the code review process is perceived by someone who is just starting in the software world and how it can evolve as a concept during the experience growth. Another important point to investigate is to relate the importance of the code review process with experience, this shows how this process is presented to the juniors and if the importance is increased or decreased throughout the years.

5.2 Survey

(Frenkli)

We used LimeSurvey to conduct the survey and collect the quantitative data. There were 20 participants with different levels of experience. We used Google Sheets to analyze the data in order to generate graphs and charts.

We started the survey with the question that asks the participants if they have ever been part of the code review. This questions’ objective is to give more insight into the code review process so the participants’ will have a clear idea of what this process is (Q1). The questions Q2 - Q4 were used to get an idea of how important do the participants think that code review process is and check if they find it as a critical process or not (Q4). We found out that every participant has been part of a code review process before or actually being part of it frequently. We can see in Figure 1, which is a bar chart that shows the involvment in a code review process based on years of experience, that no developer answered "Never". This means that this process will always be part of software developers or engineers. Only 2 participants out of 20 answered with "Neutral" in Q2 which asks them if this process has helped you improve the code quality. All the other participants answered with "Agree" or "Strongly Agree".

In order to check if the participants find code review process critical, we asked them Q4 and we found out that nearly half of them don't find it as critical. Figure 2 shows the answers for 2 questions: (a) has code review been helpful, Q2; (b) is code review critical for you, Q4. We can see that developers find the code review process important but not critical since a large number of answers are "Disagree" or "Neutral".

We also wanted to investigate which of 3 code review type is used the most. We found out that Peer Review is the most commonly used review type. As we can see in Figure 3, 89.5% of participants have used Peer Review. This type is the traditional one for reviewing code and it is still the most used one. The participants may have little knowledge of other review types thus using the traditional one.

We asked the participants if they have ever used peer review or automated review and see how effective they find it (Q5 and Q6). These questions were used to compare the two most used code review types and fulfill one of our objectives to see which tools and methods are most common in the code review process. By the data shown in Figure 4, we see that the answers are fairly distributed. We can say that Peer Review is more effective than Automated Review but there are a lot of "No Answer" in Automated Review which means that the participants don't have any knowledge about this type of review.

In Q7, we asked participants how much time it takes to implement the changes after a code review session. Our intention with this question was to indirectly see how important and serious is the code review process because if it takes a huge amount of time then it means that this process is used for serious purposes not only for best coding practices and styles. The answers are distributed in this question as we can see in Figure 5. This means that code review process is taken seriously and there can be changes from a code review that can take a lot of time compared to the total time needed to implement the feature.

We included a biased question (Q8) to see if the participants have a clear understanding of the importance of code review or not. We said that if the code review changes take up a lot of time, then the code review process is not effective and then we asked them to give a reasonable time range. If they gave a high range then we can say that the code review process is seen as extremely important by the developers. We can say that the bias had its effect on the answers as we can see in Figure 6. There are only 5.3% of participants that answered with >100% of the time. Meanwhile, the code review changes can be really important and the time needed to make this changes has no importance.

5.3 Interview

(Rabinson)

We collected qualitative data via interviews. There were 5 interviewees in total. The interview was conducted using computer-mediated communication, specifically Zoom. Following are the series of questions asked to our interviewees.

The first question was to know if their understanding of the code review process and our research topic matches. All of them had similar answers from which we took keywords and presented them in the word cloud in Figure 7. Also, everyone agreed that the process helps them expand their skills. For the mid-level developer, it was helpful because the senior's guidance could make their code quality better. For seniors, it was more about witnessing different perspectives of developing solutions to the same problem.

For daily use, GitLab was the most popular tool for reviewing codes, among them. Whereas, SonarQube was suggested as an automated tool that could help in the code review process for making it more effective.Some other tools that were mentioned in the interview are in Figure 8. One interviewee suggested splitting issues into small cards within GitLab would help in reviewing code for other developers and make it easier to modify codes. Another interviewee even claimed that this automated tool helped shortens the time for the review process.

We asked if they agree that the code review process shortens the time in the long run even it's time-consuming at the beginning. We got mixed answers on this. Some said that it is or should (if not) already be in the planning of the development. Hence the review is not a factor for time constraints but rather a standard process for quality code. Others agreed with the proposed statement saying that the possible bug is already detected to it save the struggle and time for fixing it. Apart from the tools, pair programming was also advised to be a method that would increase productivity. One interviewee suggested skipping the minor fixes and focusing more on the bigger picture (on security vulnerabilities, memory leakage, readability, etc).
